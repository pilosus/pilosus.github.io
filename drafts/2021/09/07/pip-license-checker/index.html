pip-license-checker: a license compliance tool

07-09-2021 09:10 | upd. 08-09-2021 18:38 | by Vitaly Samigullin | in Blog | 1445 words | 6 min to read
tags: license dependencies foss open-source

pip-license-checker is a license compliance tool. It detects license types for your project’s third-party dependencies. The tool covers the majority of popular free/libre and open-source (FLOSS) licenses of all types: public domain, permissive, copyleft. It also detects proprietory licenses/EULA as well as technical problems obtaining the license for a dependency.

Originally developed with Python packages in mind, the tools operates in two modes:

  1. Detecting license names and types for the list of Python dependencies
  2. Detecting license types for any list of dependencies with their license names

The tool supports output of some popular languange-specific license detection plugins:

  • gradle-license-plugin for Andoid (Java),
  • cocoapods-acknowledgements of iOS (Swift, ObjectiveC),
  • license-checker for JavaScript,
  • lein-licenses for Clojure (Leiningen),

as well as universal CSV format.

The tool is distributed both as a command-line tool, containirized application and a GitHub Action, so that it’s easy to use locally on you machine or incorporate into project’s CI/CD pipeline.

Takeaways

pip-license-checker is a tool to check lincense types in your project’s third-party dependencies. It covers the majority of popular FLOSS licenses. It has integration with some popular license fetching plugins. You can easily incorporate the toll into CI/CD pipeline to automate license compliance checks.

Why license checks are needed?

Nobody develops software from scratch today. Software development has evolved from artisan shop’s craft to a heavy industry assembly line. We, software developers, rely heavily on third-party code: libraries, packages, platforms, operating systems. Building something new has become, to a larger extent, assembling parts, applying some glue, implementing custom logic with the code written by someone else, someone outside of the project or the company. Most of such third-party dependencies today are freely available over the internet, both as executable code that a machine can run, and as human-readable code we, developers, can use, copy and learn from.

But does being freely available over the network mean these third-party dependencies are really free? Free as in “free lunch”? Free as in “freedom”? Most of the dependencies are distributed under popular FLOSS licenses, e.g. Apache License, MIT License, Mozilla Public License or GNU General Public License to name a few. The licenses differ on the “permissiveness/restrictiveness” scale, ranging from the public-domain-like licenses with no conditions to the licenses requiring the authors of derivative works to give away the source code and use the same or compatible license under certain curcumstances. Some dependencies are distributed as “freeware”, i.e. under proprietory end-user license agreements (EULA), usually with some permissions.

The conditions some licenses impose may be unacceptable for your personal project or your company. License violation is a legal risk. The court cases are real. Both financial and reputation losses may be high. In such cases preventing license violation is crucial to mitigate or avoid risks.

Sometimes choosing a “wrong” license for your FLOSS project may become an obstacle for its wide adoption (e.g. copyleft license for a project in a presense of many other alternative proprietory or free software projects of high quality). Checking licenses for project’s dependencies may help to understand your options when choosing the license most suitable for your goals.

Takeaways

  • Third-party dependencies freely available over the internet are usually distributed under some terms and conditions of FLOSS licenses or EULA. The conditions very in their permissiveness. Some license types may be too restrictive to be used in your project.
  • Legal implications and reputation losses of license violations may be serious.
  • License compliance checks are crucial to mitigate or avoid risks.

Why automating license compliance checks is needed?

The number of third-party dependencies (deps) in a project may be high: from tens in a tiny personal pet-project, to hundreds in a big one, to even thousands in the enterprise settings. As the project evoles the list of dependencies may change too.

The [list of popular FLOSS licenses](https://spdx.org/licenses/) is also long. Over one hundred and counting! While some licenses are short and easy to grasp, others are challenging reads requiring a lawyer qualification. Arguably, no average software developer ever read all of the FLOSS licenses that exist in the wild.

Increasing dependencies versions in the project may also result in dependencies changing their licenses. This is the most relevant for the deps at early stages of their development.

All in all, when managing project’s 3rd-party dependencies, we need not only to comply with their licenses, but do these license checks regularly, in an automated way. That requires a tool to be incorporated into project’s continuous integration and continuous delivery (CD/CD) pipeline.

This is exactly what pip-license-checker is for.

Takeaways

  • Modern projects rely on 10s, 100s or even 1000s of third-party dependencies. Their list is changing over time as projects evolve. This is too much to check licenses manually.
  • Free Software Foundation and Open Source Initiative, the two most respectful non-profit orginisations for free/libre and open-source software, approved over 100 of popular FLOSS licenses. Reading and understanding their conditions is a challenge for an average software developer. Expertise needed.
  • Dependencies themselves may change their licenses over the lifetime.
  • Automated license compliance checks are needed.

Who needs to check licenses?

Anyone who:

  • develops a proprietory project based on other FLOSS code
  • creates, contributes to, or maintains a FLOSS project

As an independent open-source developer you may want to check your project deps’ licenses to make sure the project’s license itself is in compliance with the libraries and packages it’s based on. That will help to avoid risk of reputation losses or enable to choose the right license for your project to make headway.

As a pro-profit company you may want to make sure your proprietory product doesn’t violate dependencies licenses to avoid legal risk, financial and reputation losses. As a startup you may want to have license compliance in place before an investment round, valuation or acquisition.

Why pip-license-checker?

There are dozens of tools and plugins to detetect license names for third-party dependencies used in your project. But as it has been said above, for a large number of dependencies and an ever-growing number of open source licenses, having the list of license names may bring little or no value without legal expertise applied.

pip-license-checker brings a bit of the expertise needed by detecting license types, so that a user may decide what types are unacceptable in her/his project and get alerted whenever some of these licenses found among project’s third-party dependencies.

What are license types?

Despite a large number of licenses third-party dependecies are distributed under on the internet, all of them can be splitted into a relatively small number of categories.

Copyright vs. free software licenses

At the highest level we may divide all the licenses into proprietory end-user license agreements (EULA) and free/libre and open-source (FLOSS) licenses. EULA dependencies may be freely available over the internet or even come with the source code available along with an executable object code, but their usage, distribution and modification may still be regulated in a more traditional copyright fashion: you need to ask for a permission explicitly. By contrast, free software licenses grant recipient of a piece of software rights to use, modify, redistribute.

FLOSS licenses, in turn, can be break down to more types. They differ on the “permissiveness/restrictiveness” scale, but the conditions they impose on someone who modify and redistribute.

Public-domain-like licenses

The most permissive type of FLOSS licenses is public domain equivalent licenses. Zero-Clause BSD comes to mind here. This type of FLOSS licenses has no coditions, but may be a liability waiver.

Permissive licenses requiring attribution

The next FLOSS license type is permissive licenses requiring attribution, preserving original copyright notice. Apache License and MIT License both fall under this license category.

Copyleft

Copyleft is the next big class of licenses. The licenses of this type may be also be called reciprocal as they require that redistribution or distribution of derivative works is done under the same or compatible license with the source code made available.

The copyleft licenses differ on the “permissiveness/restrictiveness” scale as well and can be break down into more fine-grained copyleft license types.

Weak copyleft

Weak copyleft licenses usually trigger the copyleft (share-alike mechanism) when original file (“file-level” weak copyleft, e.g. Mozilla Public License) or a library (“library-level” weak copyleft, e.g. GNU Lesser GPL) containing the code under the weak copyleft license is modified. Simply using or incorporating a file or a library (e.g. by linking process) into a larger work and dstribution of such a work won’t trigger the copyleft.
